Unit 4 · Induction — Topic 11

Inductance

The last topic. An inductor opposes changes in current and stores energy in its magnetic field — the magnetic counterpart of a capacitor.

Concept

When the current in a coil changes, its own flux changes, inducing an EMF that fights the change (Lenz again). This self-inductance $L$ (henries, H) is defined by

$\varepsilon=-L\dfrac{dI}{dt}.$

A large $L$ means the coil strongly resists any change in current — current in an inductor can't jump instantly.

Energy in the magnetic field

Charging an inductor stores energy $U=\tfrac12 LI^2$ in its magnetic field — exactly parallel to a capacitor's $U=\tfrac12 CV^2$. Inductor ↔ capacitor, current ↔ charge, $L$ ↔ $C$.

An RL circuit has a time constant $\tau=L/R$: current rises (or falls) exponentially over that timescale, just like charge in an RC circuit.

An LC circuit trades energy back and forth between the capacitor's electric field and the inductor's magnetic field, oscillating at angular frequency $\omega=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$ — an electrical version of a mass on a spring.

Key Formulas

$\varepsilon=-L\dfrac{dI}{dt}$Self-induced EMF (defines $L$)
$U=\tfrac12 LI^2$Energy stored in an inductor
$\tau=\dfrac{L}{R}$RL time constant
$\omega=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}},\qquad f=\dfrac{\omega}{2\pi}$LC oscillation frequency

L in henries (H)  ·  τ in seconds  ·  ω in rad/s.

Worked example Inductor energy, RL time, LC frequency

An inductor has $L=0.50\,\mathrm H$. (i) Find the energy stored when it carries $2.0\,\mathrm A$. (ii) Find the RL time constant with $R=100\,\Omega$. (iii) Find the oscillation frequency if connected to a $C=2.0\,\mu\mathrm F$ capacitor.

(i) Energy

$U=\tfrac12 LI^2=\tfrac12(0.50)(2.0)^2=1.0\,\mathrm J$.

(ii) RL time constant

$\tau=\dfrac{L}{R}=\dfrac{0.50}{100}=5.0\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm s$.

(iii) LC frequency

$\omega=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{(0.50)(2.0\times10^{-6})}}=1.0\times10^{3}\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, so $f=\dfrac{\omega}{2\pi}\approx159\,\mathrm{Hz}$.

Answer$U=1.0\,\mathrm J$, $\tau=5.0\,\mathrm{ms}$, $f\approx159\,\mathrm{Hz}$

Practice

Practice 1

An inductor of $0.50\,\mathrm H$ has its current changing at $4.0\,\mathrm{A/s}$. Find the induced emf.

$|\varepsilon|=L\dfrac{dI}{dt}=(0.50)(4.0)$.

Answer$|\varepsilon|=2.0\,\mathrm V$.

Why: $\varepsilon=-L\,dI/dt$ (magnitude $L\,dI/dt$).

Practice 2

Find the energy stored in a $2.0\,\mathrm H$ inductor carrying $3.0\,\mathrm A$.

$U=\tfrac12LI^2=\tfrac12(2.0)(3.0)^2$.

Answer$U=9.0\,\mathrm J$.

Why: $U=\tfrac12LI^2$.

Practice 3

A solenoid has $N=500$ turns, cross-section $1.0\times10^{-4}\,\mathrm m^2$, and length $0.20\,\mathrm m$. Find its self-inductance.

$L=\dfrac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l}=\dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(500)^2(1.0\times10^{-4})}{0.20}$.

Answer$L\approx1.6\times10^{-4}\,\mathrm H$.

Why: $L=\mu_0N^2A/l$ — note it grows as $N^2$.

Practice 4

An RL circuit has $L=4.0\,\mathrm H$ and $R=2.0\,\Omega$. Find the time constant.

$\tau=\dfrac{L}{R}=\dfrac{4.0}{2.0}$.

Answer$\tau=2.0\,\mathrm s$.

Why: RL time constant $\tau=L/R$.

Practice 5

In an RL circuit, $\varepsilon=12\,\mathrm V$ and $R=6.0\,\Omega$. Find the final current, and the current after one time constant.

Final: $I_\infty=\dfrac{\varepsilon}{R}=\dfrac{12}{6.0}=2.0\,\mathrm A$.

After one $\tau$: $I=0.63\,I_\infty=0.63(2.0)$.

Answer$I_\infty=2.0\,\mathrm A$; $I(\tau)\approx1.26\,\mathrm A$.

Why: current grows toward $\varepsilon/R$, reaching 63% in one $\tau$.

Practice 6

Two coils have mutual inductance $M=2.0\,\mathrm{mH}$. The current in coil 1 changes at $500\,\mathrm{A/s}$. Find the emf induced in coil 2.

$\varepsilon_2=M\dfrac{dI_1}{dt}=(2.0\times10^{-3})(500)$.

Answer$\varepsilon_2=1.0\,\mathrm V$.

Why: $\varepsilon_2=M\,dI_1/dt$.

Practice 7

An LC circuit has $L=2.0\,\mathrm H$ and $C=8.0\,\mu\mathrm F$. Find the angular frequency of oscillation.

$\omega=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{(2.0)(8.0\times10^{-6})}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1.6\times10^{-5}}}$.

Answer$\omega=250\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ ($f\approx40\,\mathrm{Hz}$).

Why: $\omega=1/\sqrt{LC}$.

Practice 8

In an LC circuit, at the instant the capacitor is fully discharged, where is the energy stored?

With $q=0$ the capacitor holds no energy; the current is then maximum.

So all the energy is in the inductor's magnetic field.

AnswerEntirely in the inductor (as $\tfrac12LI^2$).

Why: LC energy sloshes between $C$ and $L$ a quarter-cycle apart.

Practice 9

An inductor develops $6.0\,\mathrm V$ when its current changes at $3.0\,\mathrm{A/s}$. Find its inductance.

$L=\dfrac{|\varepsilon|}{dI/dt}=\dfrac{6.0}{3.0}$.

Answer$L=2.0\,\mathrm H$.

Why: invert $\varepsilon=L\,dI/dt$.

Practice 10

Just after a switch closes in an RL circuit, how does the inductor behave? And long afterward?

It resists the sudden change, so initially $I=0$ — like an open circuit.

Long after, $dI/dt\to0$ so it has no voltage — like a plain wire.

AnswerOpen circuit at $t=0$; plain wire (short) at steady state.

Why: inductors oppose current changes, not steady current.
