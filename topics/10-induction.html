Electromagnetic Induction

A changing magnetic field creates an electric field and drives a current. Faraday's law is the principle behind generators, transformers, and induction — likely a heavily-weighted final topic.

Concept

First define magnetic flux — how much field passes through a loop: $\Phi_B=BA\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec B$ and the loop's normal. Units are webers (1 Wb = 1 T·m²).

Faraday's law: an EMF is induced whenever the flux changes:

$\varepsilon=-N\dfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}.$

Flux can change three ways: change $B$, change the area $A$, or change the angle $\theta$ (rotate the loop). Any of them induces an EMF.

Lenz's law — the minus sign

The induced current flows in the direction that opposes the change in flux that created it. If flux through a loop increases, the induced current makes a field to push back; if it decreases, the current tries to maintain it. This is energy conservation in action.

A special case is motional EMF: a rod of length $L$ sliding at speed $v$ across a field $B$ sweeps out area and generates $\varepsilon=BLv$. The free charges in the rod feel the magnetic force and pile up until an electric field balances them.

Key Formulas

$\Phi_B=BA\cos\theta$Magnetic flux (Wb)
$\varepsilon=-N\dfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$Faraday's law ($N$ turns)
$\varepsilon=BLv$Motional EMF of a sliding rod
$I=\dfrac{|\varepsilon|}{R}$Induced current in a loop of resistance $R$

$\Phi_B$ in webers  ·  θ measured from the loop's normal  ·  the sign of $\varepsilon$ encodes Lenz's law.

Worked example Shrinking flux through a coil

A coil of $N=200$ turns and area $A=0.010\,\mathrm{m^2}$ sits perpendicular to a field that drops uniformly from $0.40\,\mathrm T$ to $0$ in $0.20\,\mathrm s$. Find the induced EMF, and the current if the coil's resistance is $5.0\,\Omega$.

Step 1 — rate of flux change

$\dfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}=A\dfrac{dB}{dt}=(0.010)\dfrac{0-0.40}{0.20}=-0.020\,\mathrm{Wb/s}$.

Step 2 — EMF (magnitude)

$|\varepsilon|=N\left|\dfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}\right|=200(0.020)=4.0\,\mathrm V$.

Step 3 — induced current

$I=\dfrac{|\varepsilon|}{R}=\dfrac{4.0}{5.0}=0.80\,\mathrm A$. By Lenz's law it flows so as to oppose the decrease in flux.

Answer$|\varepsilon|=4.0\,\mathrm V$, $I=0.80\,\mathrm A$

Practice

Practice 1

A loop of area $0.20\,\mathrm m^2$ lies with its normal along a uniform $0.50\,\mathrm T$ field. Find the flux through it.

$\Phi_B=BA\cos0^\circ=(0.50)(0.20)$.

Answer$\Phi_B=0.10\,\mathrm{Wb}$.

Why: $\Phi=BA\cos\theta$; here $\theta=0$.

Practice 2

A $0.50\,\mathrm m$ rod moves at $4.0\,\mathrm{m/s}$ perpendicular to a $0.20\,\mathrm T$ field. Find the motional emf.

$\varepsilon=BLv=(0.20)(0.50)(4.0)$.

Answer$\varepsilon=0.40\,\mathrm V$.

Why: motional emf $\varepsilon=BLv$.

Practice 3

The flux through a one-turn loop rises linearly from $0.20\,\mathrm{Wb}$ to $0.80\,\mathrm{Wb}$ in $0.30\,\mathrm s$. Find the induced emf.

$|\varepsilon|=\dfrac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}=\dfrac{0.80-0.20}{0.30}$.

Answer$|\varepsilon|=2.0\,\mathrm V$.

Why: emf is the rate of change of flux.

Practice 4

A field through a fixed $0.10\,\mathrm m^2$ loop changes at $0.50\,\mathrm{T/s}$. Find the induced emf (one turn).

$\varepsilon=A\dfrac{dB}{dt}=(0.10)(0.50)$.

Answer$\varepsilon=0.050\,\mathrm V$.

Why: with area fixed, $\varepsilon=A\,dB/dt$.

Practice 5

A $0.20\,\mathrm m^2$ loop of resistance $0.30\,\Omega$ sits in a field changing at $0.30\,\mathrm{T/s}$. Find the induced current.

$\varepsilon=A\dfrac{dB}{dt}=(0.20)(0.30)=0.06\,\mathrm V$.

$I=\dfrac{\varepsilon}{R}=\dfrac{0.06}{0.30}$.

Answer$I=0.20\,\mathrm A$.

Why: $\varepsilon=A\,dB/dt$, then $I=\varepsilon/R$.

Practice 6

The north pole of a bar magnet is pushed toward a conducting loop. Which way does the induced current flow, and why?

By Lenz's law the loop opposes the approach.

It makes a north pole facing the magnet (to repel it), so the current flows to create that field.

AnswerIn the direction that makes the loop repel the magnet (opposing the change).

Why: Lenz: induced effects oppose what causes them.

Practice 7

A $100$-turn coil of area $0.010\,\mathrm m^2$ rotates at $\omega=60\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ in a $0.20\,\mathrm T$ field. Find the peak emf.

$\varepsilon_0=NBA\omega=(100)(0.20)(0.010)(60)$.

Answer$\varepsilon_0=12\,\mathrm V$.

Why: a rotating loop gives $\varepsilon_0=NBA\omega$.

Practice 8

A loop in a constant $0.40\,\mathrm T$ field shrinks in area from $0.30\,\mathrm m^2$ to $0.10\,\mathrm m^2$ in $0.20\,\mathrm s$. Find the average induced emf.

$|\varepsilon|=B\dfrac{\Delta A}{\Delta t}=(0.40)\dfrac{0.30-0.10}{0.20}$.

Answer$|\varepsilon|=0.40\,\mathrm V$.

Why: changing area also changes flux: $\varepsilon=B\,\Delta A/\Delta t$.

Practice 9

A loop of area $0.020\,\mathrm m^2$ in a $0.50\,\mathrm T$ field is rotated so its normal goes from parallel to perpendicular to $\vec B$ in $0.10\,\mathrm s$. Find the average emf.

$\Delta\Phi=BA(\cos0^\circ-\cos90^\circ)=(0.50)(0.020)(1-0)=0.010\,\mathrm{Wb}$.

$|\varepsilon|=\dfrac{0.010}{0.10}$.

Answer$|\varepsilon|=0.10\,\mathrm V$.

Why: rotating changes $\cos\theta$, hence the flux.

Practice 10

A rod generates a $2.0\,\mathrm V$ motional emf and drives current through a $4.0\,\Omega$ resistance. Find the mechanical power you must supply to keep it moving steadily.

At constant speed, your input power equals the power dissipated: $P=\dfrac{\varepsilon^2}{R}=\dfrac{(2.0)^2}{4.0}$.

Answer$P=1.0\,\mathrm W$.

Why: steady speed ⇒ input power = $\varepsilon^2/R$ dissipated.
