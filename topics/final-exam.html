Question 1Topic 6 · Current & Resistance

The current through a conducting wire is kept constant. If the wire's cross-sectional area is halved, how does the drift velocity of the electrons change?

Same current squeezed through half the opening.

💡 What's really going on

The same number of electrons must pass each second, but now through a smaller doorway — so each electron has to move faster.

  1. Remains the same
  2. Halves
  3. Doubles
  4. Quadruples
Step 1 — the link

Current is $I=nqA v_\text{d}$, where $n$ (electron density) and $q$ are fixed for copper.

Step 2 — hold I constant

So $A\,v_\text{d}=\text{constant}$. They trade off inversely.

Step 3 — halve the area

$A\to\tfrac12 A \Rightarrow v_\text{d}\to 2v_\text{d}$.

Answer — CThe drift velocity doubles.

Why: in $I=nqAv_\text{d}$ with $I$ fixed, area and drift speed are inversely proportional.

Question 2Topic 6 · Current & Resistance

A steady current of $5.0\,\mathrm A$ flows through a cylindrical copper wire of radius $2.0\,\mathrm{mm}$. What is the approximate current density $J$? (Take $\pi\approx3.0$.)

Current density = current per unit cross-section.

💡 What's really going on

$J$ is just "how crowded" the current is — amps spread over the wire's face $A=\pi r^2$.

  1. $4.2\times10^{5}\ \mathrm{A/m^2}$
  2. $8.3\times10^{5}\ \mathrm{A/m^2}$
  3. $1.2\times10^{6}\ \mathrm{A/m^2}$
  4. $4.2\times10^{4}\ \mathrm{A/m^2}$
Step 1 — area

$A=\pi r^2=3.0\,(2.0\times10^{-3})^2=1.2\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm{m^2}$.

Step 2 — divide

$J=\dfrac{I}{A}=\dfrac{5.0}{1.2\times10^{-5}}\approx4.2\times10^{5}\,\mathrm{A/m^2}$.

Answer — A$J\approx4.2\times10^{5}\,\mathrm{A/m^2}$

Why: $J=I/A$ with $A=\pi r^2$; convert mm → m first.

Question 3Topic 6 · Current & Resistance

The $V\!-\!I$ graph of an ohmic material is a straight line through $(I=2\,\mathrm A,\,V=10\,\mathrm V)$ and $(I=4\,\mathrm A,\,V=20\,\mathrm V)$. What is the resistance $R$?

For an ohmic device the slope is the resistance.

💡 What's really going on

Ohm's law $V=IR$ is a straight line through the origin; its slope $V/I$ is exactly $R$.

  1. $0.2\,\Omega$
  2. $2.0\,\Omega$
  3. $5.0\,\Omega$
  4. $10.0\,\Omega$
Step 1 — slope

$R=\dfrac{\Delta V}{\Delta I}=\dfrac{20-10}{4-2}=\dfrac{10}{2}$.

Answer — C$R=5.0\,\Omega$

Why: on a $V$–$I$ plot, resistance = slope $=V/I$.

Question 4Topic 6 · Current & Resistance

Applying $12\,\mathrm V$ across a wire of length $10.0\,\mathrm m$ and cross-section $2.0\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{m^2}$ dissipates $24\,\mathrm W$. What is the material's resistivity $\rho$?

💡 What's really going on

The power tells you the resistance; the geometry then converts that resistance into the material property $\rho$.

  1. $1.2\times10^{-6}\ \Omega\!\cdot\!\mathrm m$
  2. $2.4\times10^{-7}\ \Omega\!\cdot\!\mathrm m$
  3. $1.2\times10^{-7}\ \Omega\!\cdot\!\mathrm m$
  4. $6.0\times10^{-4}\ \Omega\!\cdot\!\mathrm m$
Step 1 — resistance from power

$P=\dfrac{V^2}{R}\Rightarrow R=\dfrac{V^2}{P}=\dfrac{12^2}{24}=6.0\,\Omega$.

Step 2 — resistivity from geometry

$R=\rho\dfrac{L}{A}\Rightarrow \rho=\dfrac{RA}{L}=\dfrac{6.0\,(2.0\times10^{-6})}{10.0}=1.2\times10^{-6}\,\Omega\!\cdot\!\mathrm m$.

Answer — A$\rho=1.2\times10^{-6}\ \Omega\!\cdot\!\mathrm m$

Why: get $R$ from $V^2/P$, then invert $R=\rho L/A$.

Question 5Topic 7 · DC Circuits

Three resistors of $4.0\,\Omega$, $6.0\,\Omega$, and $12.0\,\Omega$ are connected in parallel. What is the equivalent resistance?

Same voltage across each branch.

💡 What's really going on

Adding parallel paths gives current more ways through, so the combined resistance is smaller than the smallest resistor.

  1. $2.0\,\Omega$
  2. $22.0\,\Omega$
  3. $0.5\,\Omega$
  4. $4.0\,\Omega$
Step 1 — reciprocals add

$\dfrac1{R_\text{eq}}=\dfrac14+\dfrac16+\dfrac1{12}=\dfrac{3+2+1}{12}=\dfrac{6}{12}=\dfrac12$.

Step 2 — invert

$R_\text{eq}=2.0\,\Omega$.

Answer — A$R_\text{eq}=2.0\,\Omega$ — note it is below the $4\,\Omega$ branch, as expected.

Why: parallel ⇒ add reciprocals, then flip.

Question 6Topic 7 · DC Circuits

An uncharged capacitor $C$ is in series with a resistor $R$ and a battery; the switch is closed. Once the capacitor is fully charged, what are the current and the voltage $V_C$ across the capacitor?

A charged capacitor blocks steady current.

💡 What's really going on

While charging, current flows and $V_C$ climbs. When the capacitor's voltage finally matches the battery, there is nothing left to push charge — current stops.

  1. Current is maximum, $V_C$ is zero.
  2. Current is zero, $V_C$ equals the battery emf $\varepsilon$.
  3. Current is zero, $V_C$ is zero.
  4. Current is maximum, $V_C$ equals the battery emf $\varepsilon$.
Step 1 — steady state

"Fully charged" means $q$ no longer changes, so $I=\dfrac{dq}{dt}=0$.

Step 2 — loop rule

With $I=0$ there is no drop across $R$, so the whole emf sits on the capacitor: $V_C=\varepsilon$.

Answer — BCurrent $=0$, and $V_C=\varepsilon$.

Why: at full charge a capacitor acts like an open switch ($I=0$); contrast with $t=0$, when it acts like a wire ($I$ max, $V_C=0$).

Question 7Topic 7 · DC Circuits

Two batteries, $10\,\mathrm V$ and $5\,\mathrm V$ (negligible internal resistance), are in series with opposing polarities, along with resistors $2.0\,\Omega$ and $3.0\,\Omega$ in series. What current flows?

Opposing emfs subtract.

💡 What's really going on

The batteries push against each other, so only the difference in their voltages drives the loop through the total resistance.

  1. $1.0\,\mathrm A$
  2. $3.0\,\mathrm A$
  3. $2.5\,\mathrm A$
  4. $5.0\,\mathrm A$
Step 1 — net emf

Opposing: $\varepsilon_\text{net}=10-5=5\,\mathrm V$.

Step 2 — total resistance

Series: $R=2.0+3.0=5.0\,\Omega$.

Step 3 — Ohm's law

$I=\dfrac{\varepsilon_\text{net}}{R}=\dfrac{5}{5}=1.0\,\mathrm A$.

Answer — A$I=1.0\,\mathrm A$

Why: opposing emfs ⇒ subtract; aiding ⇒ add. Then $I=\varepsilon_\text{net}/\sum R$.

Question 8Topic 7 · DC Circuits

In an RC circuit the charge $Q$ grows exponentially and reaches 63% of its maximum in $4.0\,\mathrm{ms}$. If $C=2.0\,\mu\mathrm F$, what is the resistance?

63% reached after one time constant τ.

💡 What's really going on

The "63% time" is the time constant $\tau=RC$ by definition. Knowing $\tau$ and $C$ pins down $R$.

  1. $2.0\times10^{3}\ \Omega$
  2. $4.0\times10^{3}\ \Omega$
  3. $2.0\times10^{2}\ \Omega$
  4. $8.0\times10^{3}\ \Omega$
Step 1 — identify τ

Time to reach 63% $=\tau=RC=4.0\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm s$.

Step 2 — solve for R

$R=\dfrac{\tau}{C}=\dfrac{4.0\times10^{-3}}{2.0\times10^{-6}}=2.0\times10^{3}\,\Omega$.

Answer — A$R=2.0\times10^{3}\,\Omega$

Why: 63% ↔ one $\tau$; then $R=\tau/C$.

Question 9Topic 8 · Magnetic Force

An electron moves in the $+x$ direction into a magnetic field pointing out of the page ($+z$). What is the initial direction of the magnetic force on it?

Right-hand rule, then flip for the negative charge.

💡 What's really going on

Point fingers along $\vec v$ ($+x$), curl toward $\vec B$ ($+z$): the rule gives $-y$ for a positive charge — so for the negative electron it flips to $+y$.

  1. $+y$ direction
  2. $-y$ direction
  3. $+z$ direction
  4. $-x$ direction
Step 1 — cross product

$\hat x\times\hat z=-\hat y$, so $\vec v\times\vec B$ points in $-y$.

Step 2 — negative charge

$\vec F=q\vec v\times\vec B$ with $q=-e$ flips it: $\vec F$ points in $+y$.

Answer — A$+y$ direction.

Why: do the right-hand rule for $\vec v\times\vec B$, then reverse because the electron is negative.

Question 10Topic 8 · Magnetic Force

A proton ($q=1.6\times10^{-19}\,\mathrm C$, $m=1.67\times10^{-27}\,\mathrm{kg}$) enters a $0.50\,\mathrm T$ field perpendicularly at $3.0\times10^{6}\,\mathrm{m/s}$. What is its orbital radius?

The magnetic force curves the proton into a circle.

💡 What's really going on

The magnetic force always points to the center, acting as the centripetal force — so $qvB=mv^2/r$, which solves for $r$.

  1. $0.031\,\mathrm m$
  2. $0.063\,\mathrm m$
  3. $0.126\,\mathrm m$
  4. $0.252\,\mathrm m$
Step 1 — formula

$qvB=\dfrac{mv^2}{r}\Rightarrow r=\dfrac{mv}{qB}$.

Step 2 — plug in

$r=\dfrac{(1.67\times10^{-27})(3.0\times10^{6})}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.50)}=\dfrac{5.0\times10^{-21}}{8.0\times10^{-20}}\approx0.063\,\mathrm m$.

Answer — B$r\approx0.063\,\mathrm m$

Why: magnetic force = centripetal force ⇒ $r=mv/qB$.

Question 11Topic 10 · Induction (flux)

A loop of area $0.40\,\mathrm{m^2}$ sits in a uniform $2.0\,\mathrm T$ field. The angle between the loop's normal and $\vec B$ is $60^\circ$. What is the magnetic flux?

Flux counts only the field component along the normal.

💡 What's really going on

Flux is "how much field pokes straight through" the loop, so it uses $\cos\theta$ with the angle to the normal.

  1. $0.0\,\mathrm{Wb}$
  2. $0.4\,\mathrm{Wb}$
  3. $0.69\,\mathrm{Wb}$
  4. $0.8\,\mathrm{Wb}$
Step 1 — formula

$\Phi_B=BA\cos\theta$ with $\theta=60^\circ$ (angle to the normal).

Step 2 — plug in

$\Phi_B=(2.0)(0.40)\cos60^\circ=0.8\times0.5=0.40\,\mathrm{Wb}$.

Answer — B$\Phi_B=0.4\,\mathrm{Wb}$

Why: $\theta$ is measured from the normal, so use $\cos$ (not $\sin$).

Question 12Topic 8 · Magnetic Force

A particle passes undeflected through crossed fields: $E=4.0\times10^{4}\,\mathrm{V/m}$ and $B$ are mutually perpendicular, and the speed is $2.0\times10^{5}\,\mathrm{m/s}$. What is $B$?

Electric and magnetic forces cancel.

💡 What's really going on

"Undeflected" means the electric and magnetic forces exactly cancel: $qE=qvB$, so $B=E/v$ — the speed drops out of $q$.

  1. $0.20\,\mathrm T$
  2. $0.40\,\mathrm T$
  3. $5.0\,\mathrm T$
  4. $8.0\,\mathrm T$
Step 1 — balance

$qE=qvB\Rightarrow B=\dfrac{E}{v}$.

Step 2 — plug in

$B=\dfrac{4.0\times10^{4}}{2.0\times10^{5}}=0.20\,\mathrm T$.

Answer — A$B=0.20\,\mathrm T$

Why: velocity selector ⇒ $E=vB$, independent of charge or mass.

Question 13Topic 9 · Sources of B

What is the magnetic field $5.0\,\mathrm{cm}$ ($0.05\,\mathrm m$) from an infinitely long straight wire carrying $10\,\mathrm A$? ($\mu_0=4\pi\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{T\!\cdot\! m/A}$.)

Field circles the wire; it weakens as 1/r.

💡 What's really going on

A straight wire wraps the field in circles around itself; its strength falls off like $1/r$.

  1. $2.0\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm T$
  2. $4.0\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm T$
  3. $2.0\times10^{-4}\,\mathrm T$
  4. $4.0\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm T$
Step 1 — formula

$B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$.

Step 2 — plug in

$B=\dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(10)}{2\pi(0.05)}=\dfrac{4\times10^{-6}}{0.1}=4.0\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm T$.

Answer — B$B=4.0\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm T$

Why: straight wire ⇒ $B=\mu_0 I/2\pi r$; the $\pi$'s cancel.

Question 14Topic 9 · Sources of B

Two very long parallel wires a distance $d$ apart carry currents $I_1$ and $I_2$ in the same direction. What is the magnetic force between them?

Same-direction currents pull together.

💡 What's really going on

Each wire sits in the other's field. Parallel (same-way) currents attract; anti-parallel currents repel.

  1. The wires attract each other.
  2. The wires repel each other.
  3. The force is perpendicular to the wires, causing no attraction/repulsion.
  4. Since the currents are in the same direction, the force is zero.
Step 1 — field of wire 1

Wire 1 makes a field at wire 2; apply $\vec F=I_2\vec L\times\vec B_1$.

Step 2 — direction

The right-hand rule gives a force pointing from each wire toward the other.

Answer — AThey attract. (Force per length $=\dfrac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi d}$.)

Why: remember "parallel currents attract, anti-parallel repel."

Question 15Topic 9 · Sources of B

In the Biot–Savart law, the field $d\vec B$ from a small current element $d\vec\ell$ points along the cross product of which two vectors?

dB ∝ d𝓵 × r̂.

💡 What's really going on

Biot–Savart is $d\vec B\propto \dfrac{I\,d\vec\ell\times\hat r}{r^2}$ — the element direction crossed into the line pointing to the field point.

  1. $d\vec\ell$ and the position vector $\vec r$
  2. The position vector $\vec r$ and the current $I$
  3. $d\vec\ell$ and the magnetic field itself
  4. The position vector $\vec r$ and the electric field $\vec E$
The law

$d\vec B=\dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{I\,d\vec\ell\times\hat r}{r^2}$ — the cross product is $d\vec\ell\times\hat r$.

Answer — A$d\vec\ell$ and the position vector $\vec r$.

Why: the only vectors in Biot–Savart are the element $d\vec\ell$ and the direction $\hat r$ to the point.

Question 16Topic 9 · Sources of B

An ideal solenoid of length $0.50\,\mathrm m$ has $1000$ turns. What current gives a center field of $3.14\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm T$? (Take $\pi\approx3.14$, $\mu_0=4\pi\times10^{-7}$.)

Uniform field inside: B = μ₀ n I.

💡 What's really going on

Inside a long solenoid the field is uniform and set by the turn density $n=N/L$ times the current.

  1. $0.50\,\mathrm A$
  2. $1.25\,\mathrm A$
  3. $2.50\,\mathrm A$
  4. $5.00\,\mathrm A$
Step 1 — turn density

$n=\dfrac{N}{L}=\dfrac{1000}{0.50}=2000\,\mathrm{turns/m}$.

Step 2 — solve B = μ₀ n I

$I=\dfrac{B}{\mu_0 n}=\dfrac{3.14\times10^{-3}}{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(2000)}=\dfrac{3.14\times10^{-3}}{2.512\times10^{-3}}=1.25\,\mathrm A$.

Answer — B$I=1.25\,\mathrm A$

Why: solenoid ⇒ $B=\mu_0 nI$ with $n=N/L$.

Question 17Topic 10 · Induction

The flux through a stationary loop is increasing with time. By Lenz's law, what is the direction of the field created by the induced current inside the loop?

Induced field fights the change.

💡 What's really going on

Induced currents are contrarian: if the field through the loop is growing, the loop pushes back with a field in the opposite direction to slow the change.

  1. It is in the same direction as the external field.
  2. It is in the opposite direction to the external field.
  3. It is perpendicular to the external field.
  4. It is independent of the external field.
Lenz's law

The induced effect always opposes the change in flux. The flux is increasing, so the induced field points opposite to $\vec B_\text{ext}$ to oppose the growth.

Answer — BOpposite to the external field.

Why: Lenz = "oppose the change"; rising flux ⇒ induced field opposes it.

Question 18Topic 10 · Induction

A conducting rod of length $0.50\,\mathrm m$ is pulled at a constant $10\,\mathrm{m/s}$ perpendicular to a uniform $0.80\,\mathrm T$ field. What emf appears between its ends?

Motional emf from sweeping field lines.

💡 What's really going on

The rod sweeps through field lines; charges in it feel a magnetic push that piles up at the ends, creating a voltage $\varepsilon=BLv$.

  1. $2.0\,\mathrm V$
  2. $4.0\,\mathrm V$
  3. $8.0\,\mathrm V$
  4. $16.0\,\mathrm V$
Step 1 — formula

$\varepsilon=BLv$.

Step 2 — plug in

$\varepsilon=(0.80)(0.50)(10)=4.0\,\mathrm V$.

Answer — B$\varepsilon=4.0\,\mathrm V$

Why: straight motional emf ⇒ $\varepsilon=BLv$ (fields ⟂ motion).

Question 19Topic 10 · Induction

For a single-turn loop, the flux rises linearly from $0.10\,\mathrm{Wb}$ to $0.50\,\mathrm{Wb}$ between $t=0$ and $t=2.0\,\mathrm s$. What is the magnitude of the induced emf?

Constant slope ⇒ constant emf.

💡 What's really going on

emf is the rate the flux changes — the slope of the $\Phi$–$t$ line. A straight line means a steady emf.

  1. $0.10\,\mathrm V$
  2. $0.20\,\mathrm V$
  3. $0.40\,\mathrm V$
  4. $0.50\,\mathrm V$
Step 1 — Faraday

$|\varepsilon|=\left|\dfrac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}\right|$ for one turn.

Step 2 — plug in

$|\varepsilon|=\dfrac{0.50-0.10}{2.0}=\dfrac{0.40}{2.0}=0.20\,\mathrm V$.

Answer — B$|\varepsilon|=0.20\,\mathrm V$

Why: emf = slope of $\Phi$ vs $t$ (times $N$, here $N=1$).

Question 20Topic 10 · Induction

A circular loop of radius $0.10\,\mathrm m$ and resistance $0.50\,\Omega$ sits perpendicular to a field that grows at $dB/dt=0.20\,\mathrm{T/s}$. What is the induced current? (Take $\pi\approx3.0$.)

Changing field drives a current around the loop.

💡 What's really going on

A changing field makes an emf $=A\,dB/dt$; Ohm's law then turns that emf into a current through the loop's resistance.

  1. $0.006\,\mathrm A$
  2. $0.012\,\mathrm A$
  3. $0.024\,\mathrm A$
  4. $0.120\,\mathrm A$
Step 1 — emf

$\varepsilon=A\dfrac{dB}{dt}=\pi r^2\dfrac{dB}{dt}=3.0(0.10)^2(0.20)=6.0\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm V$.

Step 2 — Ohm's law

$I=\dfrac{\varepsilon}{R}=\dfrac{6.0\times10^{-3}}{0.50}=0.012\,\mathrm A$.

Answer — B$I=0.012\,\mathrm A$

Why: $\varepsilon=A\,dB/dt$, then $I=\varepsilon/R$.
