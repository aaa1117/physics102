The Electric Field

Instead of asking "what force does charge A feel from charge B," we say every charge fills space with a field. Any charge placed in that field feels a force. This shift makes every later topic possible.

Concept

The electric field $\vec E$ at a point is the force per unit positive charge that a tiny test charge would feel there: $\vec E=\vec F/q_0$. Its units are newtons per coulomb (N/C), equivalently volts per metre. The field points the way a positive charge would be pushed: away from positive source charges, toward negative ones.

Why a field?

A field lets you describe the influence of a charge distribution once, then find the force on any charge with a single multiplication $\vec F=q\vec E$. It also carries energy and travels at finite speed ÔÇö the seed of electromagnetic waves.

Superposition again rules: the total field is the vector sum of the fields from each source charge. For a continuous charge distribution you slice it into tiny pieces $dq$, write $d\vec E=k\,dq/r^2\,\hat r$, and integrate.

Field lines are a picture of $\vec E$: they start on positive charge, end on negative charge, never cross, and crowd together where the field is strong.

Key Formulas

$\vec E=\dfrac{\vec F}{q_0}\qquad \vec F=q\vec E$Definition of the field; force on a charge in a field
$E=k\dfrac{|q|}{r^2}$Field magnitude of a point charge
$\vec E_\text{net}=\textstyle\sum_i k\dfrac{q_i}{r_i^2}\,\hat r_i$Superposition of point-charge fields
$E=\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}\ \text{(sheet)},\quad E=\dfrac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}\ \text{(line)}$Useful continuous distributions

Watch out

Field is a vector ÔÇö you cannot add magnitudes unless the contributions point the same way. Break each contribution into components, sum components, then recombine.

Worked example Field on the axis between two charges

A charge $+4.0\,\mathrm{nC}$ is at $x=0$ and a charge $-4.0\,\mathrm{nC}$ is at $x=0.30\,\mathrm m$. Find $\vec E$ at the midpoint $x=0.15\,\mathrm m$.

Step 1 ÔÇö distances

Each charge is $r=0.15\,\mathrm m$ from the midpoint.

Step 2 ÔÇö each field

$E=k\dfrac{|q|}{r^2}=8.99\times10^{9}\dfrac{4.0\times10^{-9}}{(0.15)^2}=1.6\times10^{3}\,\mathrm{N/C}$ each.

Step 3 ÔÇö directions

Field from $+4$ nC points away ÔåÆ $+x$. Field from $-4$ nC points toward it ÔåÆ also $+x$. They add.

Answer$\vec E=3.2\times10^{3}\ \mathrm{N/C}$ in the $+x$ direction

Practice

Practice 1

Find the electric field $0.30\,\mathrm m$ from a $+5.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ point charge.

$E=\dfrac{kq}{r^2}=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(5.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.30)^2}=\dfrac{4.5\times10^4}{0.09}$.

Answer$E=5.0\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}$, pointing away from the charge.

Why: $E=kq/r^2$; direction is radially outward for $+q$.

Practice 2

A uniform field $E=2000\,\mathrm{N/C}$ points in $+x$. Find the force (magnitude and direction) on an electron in it.

$F=qE=(1.6\times10^{-19})(2000)=3.2\times10^{-16}\,\mathrm N$.

The charge is negative, so the force is opposite to $\vec E$.

Answer$F=3.2\times10^{-16}\,\mathrm N$ in the $-x$ direction.

Why: $\vec F=q\vec E$; negative charge ÔçÆ force opposite the field.

Practice 3

A dipole has $+6.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ at $x=0$ and $-6.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ at $x=0.60\,\mathrm m$. Find the field at the midpoint.

Each charge is $0.30\,\mathrm m$ away: $E=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(6\times10^{-6})}{0.09}=6.0\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}$.

Both fields point toward the $-$ charge ($+x$), so they add.

Answer$E=1.2\times10^6\,\mathrm{N/C}$ toward the negative charge.

Why: between $+$ and $-$, the two fields reinforce.

Practice 4

$+Q$ is at $x=0$ and $+4Q$ is at $x=0.30\,\mathrm m$. Where between them is the field zero?

$\dfrac{Q}{x^2}=\dfrac{4Q}{(0.30-x)^2}\Rightarrow\dfrac{0.30-x}{x}=2$.

$0.30-x=2x\Rightarrow x=0.10\,\mathrm m$.

Answer$0.10\,\mathrm m$ from the smaller ($+Q$) charge.

Why: set the two field magnitudes equal; null sits nearer the smaller charge.

Practice 5

Two equal positive charges sit a distance $2a$ apart. What is the field exactly at the midpoint, and why?

The two fields have equal magnitude but point in opposite directions.

They cancel.

Answer$E=0$ at the midpoint, by symmetry.

Why: equal-and-opposite contributions cancel.

Practice 6

A dipole points from the $-$ charge toward the $+$ charge. In what direction does $\vec E$ point at a location on the perpendicular bisector?

On the bisector the components toward each charge cancel; the surviving part points from $+$ to $-$.

That is, antiparallel to the dipole moment $\vec p$.

AnswerAntiparallel to $\vec p$ (from the $+$ charge toward the $-$ charge side).

Why: on the bisector, $\vec E$ is opposite to the dipole moment.

Practice 7

True or false: electric field lines can cross one another. Explain.

At any point the field has a single, well-defined direction.

Two crossing lines would give two directions there ÔÇö impossible.

AnswerFalse ÔÇö field lines never cross.

Why: the field is single-valued at each point.

Practice 8

Find the acceleration of an electron in a uniform field of $500\,\mathrm{N/C}$.

$a=\dfrac{qE}{m}=\dfrac{(1.6\times10^{-19})(500)}{9.11\times10^{-31}}=\dfrac{8.0\times10^{-17}}{9.11\times10^{-31}}$.

Answer$a\approx8.8\times10^{13}\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$.

Why: $a=qE/m$ ÔÇö tiny mass ÔçÆ enormous acceleration.

Practice 9

A proton starts from rest and is pushed through $0.10\,\mathrm m$ by a uniform field $E=2.0\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}$. Find its final speed.

$a=\dfrac{qE}{m}=\dfrac{(1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^4)}{1.67\times10^{-27}}=1.92\times10^{12}\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$.

$v=\sqrt{2as}=\sqrt{2(1.92\times10^{12})(0.10)}$.

Answer$v\approx6.2\times10^5\,\mathrm{m/s}$.

Why: $a=qE/m$, then kinematics $v^2=2as$.

Practice 10

At $0.10\,\mathrm m$ from a point charge the field is $3.0\times10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}$. Find the charge.

$q=\dfrac{Er^2}{k}=\dfrac{(3.0\times10^4)(0.10)^2}{9.0\times10^9}=\dfrac{300}{9.0\times10^9}$.

Answer$q\approx3.3\times10^{-8}\,\mathrm C$ (33 nC).

Why: invert $E=kq/r^2$ for $q$.
