The Electric Field

Instead of asking "what force does charge A feel from charge B," we say every charge fills space with a field. Any charge placed in that field feels a force. This shift makes every later topic possible.

Concept

The electric field $\vec E$ at a point is the force per unit positive charge that a tiny test charge would feel there: $\vec E=\vec F/q_0$. Its units are newtons per coulomb (N/C), equivalently volts per metre. The field points the way a positive charge would be pushed: away from positive source charges, toward negative ones.

Why a field?

A field lets you describe the influence of a charge distribution once, then find the force on any charge with a single multiplication $\vec F=q\vec E$. It also carries energy and travels at finite speed — the seed of electromagnetic waves.

Superposition again rules: the total field is the vector sum of the fields from each source charge. For a continuous charge distribution you slice it into tiny pieces $dq$, write $d\vec E=k\,dq/r^2\,\hat r$, and integrate.

Field lines are a picture of $\vec E$: they start on positive charge, end on negative charge, never cross, and crowd together where the field is strong.

Key Formulas

$\vec E=\dfrac{\vec F}{q_0}\qquad \vec F=q\vec E$Definition of the field; force on a charge in a field
$E=k\dfrac{|q|}{r^2}$Field magnitude of a point charge
$\vec E_\text{net}=\textstyle\sum_i k\dfrac{q_i}{r_i^2}\,\hat r_i$Superposition of point-charge fields
$E=\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}\ \text{(sheet)},\quad E=\dfrac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}\ \text{(line)}$Useful continuous distributions

σ = surface charge density (C/m²)  ·  λ = linear charge density (C/m)  ·  q₀ = test charge.

Watch out

Field is a vector — you cannot add magnitudes unless the contributions point the same way. Break each contribution into components, sum components, then recombine.

Worked example Field on the axis between two charges

A charge $+4.0\,\mathrm{nC}$ is at $x=0$ and a charge $-4.0\,\mathrm{nC}$ is at $x=0.30\,\mathrm m$. Find $\vec E$ at the midpoint $x=0.15\,\mathrm m$.

Step 1 — distances

Each charge is $r=0.15\,\mathrm m$ from the midpoint.

Step 2 — each field

$E=k\dfrac{|q|}{r^2}=8.99\times10^{9}\dfrac{4.0\times10^{-9}}{(0.15)^2}=1.6\times10^{3}\,\mathrm{N/C}$ each.

Step 3 — directions

Field from $+4$ nC points away → $+x$. Field from $-4$ nC points toward it → also $+x$. They add.

Answer$\vec E=3.2\times10^{3}\ \mathrm{N/C}$ in the $+x$ direction

Practice

Practice 1

What is the magnitude of the electric field $0.50\,\mathrm m$ from a $+2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ point charge? What force would a $-1.0\,\mathrm{nC}$ charge feel there?

$E=k\dfrac{q}{r^2}=8.99\times10^{9}\dfrac{2.0\times10^{-6}}{0.25}=7.2\times10^{4}\,\mathrm{N/C}$ (away from the charge).

$F=|q|E=(1.0\times10^{-9})(7.2\times10^{4})=7.2\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm N$, directed toward the source (negative charge).

Answer$E\approx7.2\times10^{4}\,\mathrm{N/C}$, $F\approx7.2\times10^{-5}\,\mathrm N$

Practice 2

Two equal charges $+q$ sit at $x=\pm a$. Explain why $\vec E=0$ at the origin, and where the field would not be zero.

At the origin each charge produces a field of the same magnitude $kq/a^2$ but pointing in opposite directions (each points away from its source, i.e. toward the origin's opposite side). They cancel: $\vec E=0$.

Anywhere off the centre — e.g. on the y-axis — the two contributions no longer cancel; the x-components cancel by symmetry but the y-components add, giving a field pointing away from the axis.

AnswerCancellation at the centre by symmetry; nonzero elsewhere (e.g. along $y$, pointing $\pm y$).
