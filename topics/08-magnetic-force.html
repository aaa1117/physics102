Magnetic Field & Force

Moving charges feel a sideways force in a magnetic field. The cross product and the right-hand rule are everything here.

Concept

A magnetic field $\vec B$ (tesla, T) exerts a force on a moving charge — but only on the part of the velocity perpendicular to $\vec B$. The force is

$\vec F=q\,\vec v\times\vec B,\qquad F=qvB\sin\theta.$

The force is always perpendicular to both $\vec v$ and $\vec B$. Because it's perpendicular to the motion, it does no work and never changes the speed — only the direction.

Right-hand rule

Point your fingers along $\vec v$, curl them toward $\vec B$; your thumb gives $\vec v\times\vec B$. For a positive charge that's the force direction; for a negative charge, reverse it.

A charge moving perpendicular to a uniform $\vec B$ goes in a circle: the magnetic force supplies the centripetal force, $qvB=\dfrac{mv^2}{r}$, giving radius $r=\dfrac{mv}{qB}$.

A current is just moving charge, so a wire of length $L$ carrying current $I$ feels $\vec F=I\vec L\times\vec B$. A current loop in a field feels a torque that tries to align it — the principle of the electric motor.

Key Formulas

$F=qvB\sin\theta$Force on a moving charge
$r=\dfrac{mv}{qB}$Radius of circular motion
$F=BIL\sin\theta$Force on a current-carrying wire
$\tau=NIAB\sin\theta$Torque on an $N$-turn loop of area $A$

B in tesla (T)  ·  θ = angle between $\vec v$ (or wire) and $\vec B$  ·  A = loop area (m²).

Watch out

If $\vec v\parallel\vec B$ ($\theta=0$) the force is zero. Maximum force is at $\theta=90^\circ$. And don't forget to flip the direction for negative charges.

Worked example Proton in a magnetic field

A proton ($q=1.6\times10^{-19}\,\mathrm C$, $m=1.67\times10^{-27}\,\mathrm{kg}$) moves at $v=2.0\times10^{6}\,\mathrm{m/s}$ perpendicular to a $0.50\,\mathrm T$ field. Find the force and the radius of its circular path.

Step 1 — force

$F=qvB\sin90^\circ=(1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^{6})(0.50)=1.6\times10^{-13}\,\mathrm N$.

Step 2 — radius

$r=\dfrac{mv}{qB}=\dfrac{(1.67\times10^{-27})(2.0\times10^{6})}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.50)}=4.2\times10^{-2}\,\mathrm m$.

Answer$F=1.6\times10^{-13}\,\mathrm N$, $r\approx4.2\,\mathrm{cm}$

Practice

Practice 1

A wire of length $0.25\,\mathrm m$ carries $4.0\,\mathrm A$ perpendicular to a $0.30\,\mathrm T$ field. What force acts on it?

$F=BIL\sin90^\circ=(0.30)(4.0)(0.25)=0.30\,\mathrm N$.

Answer$F=0.30\,\mathrm N$

Practice 2

An electron moves parallel to a magnetic field. What magnetic force does it feel, and why?

$F=qvB\sin\theta$ with $\theta=0^\circ$, so $\sin\theta=0$ and $F=0$. The magnetic force only acts on the component of velocity perpendicular to $\vec B$; here there is none.

AnswerZero force ($\vec v\parallel\vec B$).
