Electric Potential & Energy

Potential is "electrical height" — energy per unit charge. It turns vector field problems into easy scalar arithmetic and powers every circuit in this course.

Concept

Moving a charge through a field does work and changes its potential energy $U$. Dividing by the charge gives the electric potential $V=U/q$, measured in volts (1 V = 1 J/C). Only differences in potential matter physically.

Because $V$ is a scalar, the potential of several charges is just the algebraic sum $V=\sum k q_i/r_i$ — no vectors, no angles. A positive charge released in a field "falls" from high $V$ to low $V$, gaining kinetic energy.

Field ↔ potential

For a uniform field (like between parallel plates) the link is simply $E=\dfrac{V}{d}$, and the energy a charge gains crossing the gap is $\Delta U=q\,\Delta V$. This is the engine behind Q3 of the midterm.

The electron-volt (eV) is the energy one elementary charge gains across 1 volt: $1\,\mathrm{eV}=1.6\times10^{-19}\,\mathrm J$. For a charge of magnitude $e$ crossing a potential difference of $V$ volts, the energy in eV is just the number $V$.

Key Formulas

$V=k\dfrac{q}{r}\qquad V_\text{net}=\textstyle\sum_i k\dfrac{q_i}{r_i}$Potential of point charges (scalar sum)
$U=k\dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r}\qquad \Delta U=q\,\Delta V$Pair energy; energy change of a charge
$E=\dfrac{V}{d}$Uniform field between plates ($d$ = gap)
$\text{KE}_\text{gained}=q\,\Delta V$Work–energy theorem in a field

$1\,\mathrm{V}=1\,\mathrm{J/C}$  ·  $1\,\mathrm{eV}=1.6\times10^{-19}\,\mathrm J$  ·  proton charge $=+e$, mass $=1.67\times10^{-27}\,\mathrm{kg}$.

Midterm Q3 Proton across parallel plates

A potential difference $V=80\,\mathrm V$ is applied across two parallel plates separated by $d=4.0\,\mathrm{mm}=4.0\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm m$. A proton is released from the positive plate.

(a) Field between the plates

$E=\dfrac{V}{d}=\dfrac{80}{4.0\times10^{-3}}=2.0\times10^{4}\,\mathrm{V/m}$.

Answer (a)$E=2.0\times10^{4}\ \mathrm{V/m}$
(b) Kinetic energy at the negative plate

All the electrical PE converts to KE: $\text{KE}=qV=e\,V=(1.6\times10^{-19})(80)=1.28\times10^{-17}\,\mathrm J$. In eV: $80\,\mathrm{eV}$.

Answer (b)$\text{KE}=1.28\times10^{-17}\ \mathrm J = 80\ \mathrm{eV}$
(c) Work done by the electric force

The work equals the KE gained (energy conservation), so it's identical to part (b).

Answer (c)$W=1.28\times10^{-17}\ \mathrm J = 80\ \mathrm{eV}$

Booklet B ($V=60\,\mathrm V$)

(a) $E=1.5\times10^{4}\,\mathrm{V/m}$   (b),(c) $\text{KE}=W=9.6\times10^{-18}\,\mathrm J=60\,\mathrm{eV}$.

Practice

Practice 1

Find the potential $0.30\,\mathrm m$ from a $+4.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ charge.

$V=\dfrac{kq}{r}=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(4.0\times10^{-6})}{0.30}$.

Answer$V=1.2\times10^5\,\mathrm V$.

Why: $V=kq/r$ (a scalar — no direction).

Practice 2

Find the potential energy of $+2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ and $+3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ held $0.20\,\mathrm m$ apart.

$U=\dfrac{kq_1q_2}{r}=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(2\times10^{-6})(3\times10^{-6})}{0.20}$.

Answer$U=0.27\,\mathrm J$.

Why: $U=kq_1q_2/r$; positive ⇒ work was needed to assemble.

Practice 3

A point is $0.10\,\mathrm m$ from a $+3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ charge and $0.20\,\mathrm m$ from a $+1.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ charge. Find the total potential there.

$V=k\!\left(\dfrac{3\times10^{-6}}{0.10}+\dfrac{1\times10^{-6}}{0.20}\right)=9.0\times10^9(3\times10^{-5}+5\times10^{-6})$.

Answer$V\approx3.2\times10^5\,\mathrm V$.

Why: potentials add as scalars (just sum them).

Practice 4

How much work moves $+3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ from a point at $100\,\mathrm V$ to a point at $300\,\mathrm V$?

$W=q\,\Delta V=(3.0\times10^{-6})(300-100)$.

Answer$W=6.0\times10^{-4}\,\mathrm J$.

Why: $W=q\Delta V$; positive charge to higher $V$ needs work in.

Practice 5

Two parallel plates are $0.020\,\mathrm m$ apart with $200\,\mathrm V$ across them. Find the field between them.

$E=\dfrac{V}{d}=\dfrac{200}{0.020}$.

Answer$E=1.0\times10^4\,\mathrm{V/m}$.

Why: uniform field ⇒ $E=V/d$.

Practice 6

An electron is accelerated from rest through $100\,\mathrm V$. Find its final speed.

$qV=\tfrac12mv^2\Rightarrow v=\sqrt{\dfrac{2qV}{m}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2(1.6\times10^{-19})(100)}{9.11\times10^{-31}}}$.

Answer$v\approx5.9\times10^6\,\mathrm{m/s}$.

Why: electrical PE converts fully to KE: $qV=\tfrac12mv^2$.

Practice 7

An electron is accelerated through $5000\,\mathrm V$. Express its energy gain in joules.

Energy $=qV=5000\,\mathrm{eV}=(5000)(1.6\times10^{-19})\,\mathrm J$.

Answer$8.0\times10^{-16}\,\mathrm J$ (i.e. $5\,\mathrm{keV}$).

Why: $1\,\mathrm{eV}=1.6\times10^{-19}\,\mathrm J$.

Practice 8

Four $+2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ charges sit at the corners of a square of side $0.20\,\mathrm m$. Find the potential at the centre.

Corner-to-centre distance $=\dfrac{0.20}{\sqrt2}=0.141\,\mathrm m$.

$V=4\cdot\dfrac{kq}{r}=4\cdot\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(2\times10^{-6})}{0.141}$.

Answer$V\approx5.1\times10^5\,\mathrm V$.

Why: add the four equal scalar potentials.

Practice 9

Three $+1.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ charges sit at the corners of an equilateral triangle of side $0.10\,\mathrm m$. Find the total potential energy.

Three equal pairs: $U=3\cdot\dfrac{kq^2}{a}=3\cdot\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(1\times10^{-6})^2}{0.10}$.

Answer$U=0.27\,\mathrm J$.

Why: sum the PE of every pair ($3$ pairs here).

Practice 10

A positive charge moves on its own from a high-potential point to a low-potential point. Does its potential energy rise or fall?

$\Delta U=q\,\Delta V$ with $q>0$ and $\Delta V<0$, so $\Delta U<0$.

AnswerIt falls (the lost PE becomes kinetic energy).

Why: positive charges 'fall' from high to low potential, like a ball downhill.
