Gauss's Law & Conductors

Gauss's law turns a hard integral into a one-line calculation whenever the charge has symmetry. It's also the fastest route to understanding conductors.

Concept

The electric flux through a surface measures how much field "flows" through it: $\Phi_E=\int\vec E\cdot d\vec A$. For a flat area in a uniform field, $\Phi_E=EA\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec E$ and the surface's normal.

Gauss's law says the net flux out of any closed surface depends only on the charge enclosed:

$\displaystyle\oint\vec E\cdot d\vec A=\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$

Charge outside the surface contributes zero net flux. Choose a clever "Gaussian surface" matching the symmetry (sphere, cylinder, or pillbox) so $E$ is constant over it and comes out of the integral.

Conductors in electrostatic equilibrium — four facts

  • The field inside the conducting material is zero (charges rearrange until it is).
  • Any net charge sits on the surface.
  • A charge in a cavity induces $-q$ on the inner wall and $+q$ on the outer wall.
  • Just outside the surface, $E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0$, perpendicular to the surface.

Key Formulas

$\Phi_E=\oint\vec E\cdot d\vec A=\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$Gauss's law
$E=k\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{r^2}=\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$Spherical symmetry (field outside enclosed charge)
$E_\text{inside conductor}=0$Electrostatic equilibrium

$\Phi_E$ in $\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C}$  ·  $q_\text{enc}$ = charge inside the chosen closed surface  ·  $\varepsilon_0=8.85\times10^{-12}\,\mathrm{C^2/(N\cdot m^2)}$.

Midterm Q2 Point charge inside a conducting shell

A point charge $+Q$ sits at the centre of an uncharged conducting spherical shell of inner radius $R$ and outer radius $\tfrac{3R}{2}$.

(a) Field at $r=R/2$, and inside the metal

Draw a Gaussian sphere of radius $r=R/2$ (in the hollow cavity). It encloses only $+Q$:

$E\cdot4\pi r^2=\dfrac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}\Rightarrow E=k\dfrac{Q}{(R/2)^2}=\dfrac{4kQ}{R^2}$ (radially outward).

Inside the conducting material ($R Answer (a)$E(R/2)=\dfrac{4kQ}{R^2}$ outward;   $E_\text{in metal}=0$

(b) Flux through a sphere at $r=2R$

This surface encloses the point charge $+Q$ plus the whole (neutral) shell, so $q_\text{enc}=+Q$:

Answer (b)$\Phi_E=\dfrac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}$
(c) Charges on the inner and outer surfaces

The field inside the metal is zero, so a Gaussian sphere inside the metal must enclose zero net charge → the inner wall carries $-Q$. The shell is neutral overall, so the outer wall carries $+Q$.

Answer (c)Inner surface: $-Q$.   Outer surface: $+Q$.

Practice

Practice 1

A charge of $+8.85\,\mathrm{nC}$ is enclosed by a closed surface. What is the total electric flux through that surface?

$\Phi_E=\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{8.85\times10^{-9}}{8.85\times10^{-12}}=1.0\times10^{3}\,\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C}$. The shape of the surface doesn't matter.

Answer$\Phi_E=1.0\times10^{3}\ \mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C}$

Practice 2

A solid conducting sphere carries a charge $+Q$. What is the field at a point inside the metal, and just outside the surface (radius $R$)?

Inside the conductor: $E=0$ (all charge lives on the surface, and equilibrium demands zero internal field).

Just outside: a Gaussian sphere of radius $R$ encloses all $+Q$, so $E=k\dfrac{Q}{R^2}$, pointing radially outward — exactly as if all the charge were a point at the centre.

Answer$E_\text{inside}=0$;   $E_\text{surface}=kQ/R^2$
