Gauss's Law & Conductors

Gauss's law turns a hard integral into a one-line calculation whenever the charge has symmetry. It's also the fastest route to understanding conductors.

Concept

The electric flux through a surface measures how much field "flows" through it: $\Phi_E=\int\vec E\cdot d\vec A$. For a flat area in a uniform field, $\Phi_E=EA\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec E$ and the surface's normal.

Gauss's law says the net flux out of any closed surface depends only on the charge enclosed:

$\displaystyle\oint\vec E\cdot d\vec A=\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$

Charge outside the surface contributes zero net flux. Choose a clever "Gaussian surface" matching the symmetry (sphere, cylinder, or pillbox) so $E$ is constant over it and comes out of the integral.

Conductors in electrostatic equilibrium — four facts

  • The field inside the conducting material is zero (charges rearrange until it is).
  • Any net charge sits on the surface.
  • A charge in a cavity induces $-q$ on the inner wall and $+q$ on the outer wall.
  • Just outside the surface, $E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0$, perpendicular to the surface.

Key Formulas

$\Phi_E=\oint\vec E\cdot d\vec A=\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$Gauss's law
$E=k\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{r^2}=\dfrac{q_\text{enc}}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$Spherical symmetry (field outside enclosed charge)
$E_\text{inside conductor}=0$Electrostatic equilibrium

$\Phi_E$ in $\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C}$  ·  $q_\text{enc}$ = charge inside the chosen closed surface  ·  $\varepsilon_0=8.85\times10^{-12}\,\mathrm{C^2/(N\cdot m^2)}$.

Midterm Q2 Point charge inside a conducting shell

A point charge $+Q$ sits at the centre of an uncharged conducting spherical shell of inner radius $R$ and outer radius $\tfrac{3R}{2}$.

(a) Field at $r=R/2$, and inside the metal

Draw a Gaussian sphere of radius $r=R/2$ (in the hollow cavity). It encloses only $+Q$:

$E\cdot4\pi r^2=\dfrac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}\Rightarrow E=k\dfrac{Q}{(R/2)^2}=\dfrac{4kQ}{R^2}$ (radially outward).

Inside the conducting material ($R Answer (a)$E(R/2)=\dfrac{4kQ}{R^2}$ outward;   $E_\text{in metal}=0$

(b) Flux through a sphere at $r=2R$

This surface encloses the point charge $+Q$ plus the whole (neutral) shell, so $q_\text{enc}=+Q$:

Answer (b)$\Phi_E=\dfrac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}$
(c) Charges on the inner and outer surfaces

The field inside the metal is zero, so a Gaussian sphere inside the metal must enclose zero net charge → the inner wall carries $-Q$. The shell is neutral overall, so the outer wall carries $+Q$.

Answer (c)Inner surface: $-Q$.   Outer surface: $+Q$.

Practice

Practice 1

A charge $q=2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ is enclosed by a closed surface. Find the electric flux through it.

$\Phi_E=\dfrac{q_\text{in}}{\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{2.0\times10^{-6}}{8.85\times10^{-12}}$.

Answer$\Phi_E\approx2.3\times10^5\,\mathrm{N\!\cdot\! m^2/C}$.

Why: Gauss: flux depends only on enclosed charge.

Practice 2

A charge $q=6.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ sits at the centre of a cube. Find the flux through one face.

Total flux $=\dfrac{q}{\varepsilon_0}=6.78\times10^5\,\mathrm{N\!\cdot\! m^2/C}$.

By symmetry each of the 6 faces gets $1/6$: $1.13\times10^5$.

Answer$\Phi_\text{face}\approx1.1\times10^5\,\mathrm{N\!\cdot\! m^2/C}$.

Why: symmetry ⇒ each face carries $q/6\varepsilon_0$.

Practice 3

Find the field $0.20\,\mathrm m$ from the centre of a sphere carrying $Q=4.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ (point outside).

Outside, the sphere acts like a point charge: $E=\dfrac{kQ}{r^2}=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(4\times10^{-6})}{0.04}$.

Answer$E=9.0\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}$.

Why: outside any spherical charge, treat all charge as at the centre.

Practice 4

An infinite line carries $\lambda=3.0\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C/m}$. Find the field $0.20\,\mathrm m$ away.

$E=\dfrac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}=\dfrac{2k\lambda}{r}=\dfrac{2(9.0\times10^9)(3.0\times10^{-6})}{0.20}$.

Answer$E=2.7\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}$.

Why: line charge ⇒ $E\propto1/r$ (not $1/r^2$).

Practice 5

Find the field from an infinite sheet of charge with $\sigma=2.0\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C/m^2}$.

$E=\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{2.0\times10^{-6}}{2(8.85\times10^{-12})}$.

Answer$E\approx1.1\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}$ (uniform, independent of distance).

Why: infinite sheet ⇒ $E=\sigma/2\varepsilon_0$, same everywhere.

Practice 6

A conductor has surface charge density $\sigma=4.0\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C/m^2}$. Find the field just outside, and the field inside.

Just outside a conductor: $E=\dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{4.0\times10^{-6}}{8.85\times10^{-12}}=4.5\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}$.

Inside a conductor (electrostatics): $E=0$.

AnswerOutside $E\approx4.5\times10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}$; inside $E=0$.

Why: note the conductor value is $\sigma/\varepsilon_0$, twice the lone-sheet value.

Practice 7

A solid conducting sphere of radius $0.10\,\mathrm m$ carries $Q=5.0\,\mu\mathrm C$. Find $E$ at $r=0.05\,\mathrm m$ and at the surface.

Inside a conductor $E=0$, so at $r=0.05\,\mathrm m$, $E=0$.

At the surface: $E=\dfrac{kQ}{R^2}=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(5\times10^{-6})}{0.01}=4.5\times10^6\,\mathrm{N/C}$.

Answer$E=0$ inside; $E=4.5\times10^6\,\mathrm{N/C}$ at the surface.

Why: all charge sits on the surface ⇒ interior field zero.

Practice 8

A closed surface encloses $+5.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ and $-2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$; a $+10\,\mu\mathrm C$ charge lies outside it. Find the net flux.

Only enclosed charge counts: $q_\text{in}=5.0-2.0=3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$.

$\Phi_E=\dfrac{3.0\times10^{-6}}{8.85\times10^{-12}}$.

Answer$\Phi_E\approx3.4\times10^5\,\mathrm{N\!\cdot\! m^2/C}$ (outside charge contributes nothing).

Why: external charges give zero net flux.

Practice 9

A flat sheet of area $2.0\,\mathrm m^2$ sits in a uniform $500\,\mathrm{N/C}$ field; its normal makes $60^\circ$ with the field. Find the flux.

$\Phi_E=EA\cos\theta=500(2.0)\cos60^\circ=500(2.0)(0.5)$.

Answer$\Phi_E=500\,\mathrm{N\!\cdot\! m^2/C}$.

Why: open surface in a uniform field: $\Phi=EA\cos\theta$.

Practice 10

A point charge $+3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ sits at the centre of a neutral hollow conducting shell. What charge appears on the inner and outer surfaces?

The inner surface must cancel the enclosed field: it holds $-3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$.

The shell is neutral overall, so the outer surface holds $+3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$.

AnswerInner surface $-3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$; outer surface $+3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$.

Why: induced inner charge cancels enclosed; outer keeps the shell neutral.
