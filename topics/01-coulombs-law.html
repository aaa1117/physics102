Coulomb's Law

Electric charge is the source of all the phenomena in this course. Coulomb's law tells you the force between two charges — the foundation everything else is built on.

Concept

There are two kinds of electric charge, positive and negative, measured in coulombs (C). The rule is simple: like charges repel, opposite charges attract. Charge is quantized (always a multiple of $e=1.6\times10^{-19}\,\mathrm{C}$) and conserved (never created or destroyed, only moved).

Two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by a distance $r$ push or pull on each other with a force whose size grows with the charges and falls off with the square of the distance. That inverse-square behaviour is the heart of Coulomb's law.

The key idea

The force is along the line joining the charges. Its magnitude comes from the formula; its direction comes from the signs (repel if same, attract if opposite). Always handle size and direction separately.

When several charges act on one target, use superposition: compute the force from each source charge separately, then add the force vectors. The presence of a third charge never changes the force between the first two.

Key Formulas

$F=k\dfrac{|q_1\,q_2|}{r^2}$Magnitude of the Coulomb force
$\vec F_{2\text{ on }1}=k\dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\,\hat r$Vector form — sign of the product gives the direction along $\hat r$
$U=k\dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r}$Electric potential energy of a pair (used for separation energy)

k = $8.99\times10^{9}\,\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C^2}$ (Coulomb constant)  ·  q = charge (C)  ·  r = separation (m)  ·  F in newtons, U in joules.

Separation energy

The energy needed to pull two charges infinitely far apart equals the work against their attraction: $W=U_\infty-U_{r}=0-k\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r}=-k\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r}$. For opposite charges $U$ is negative, so this work is positive — you must supply energy.

Midterm Q1 Two charges $+5Q$ and $-Q$ on a line

Two point charges, $+5Q$ and $-Q$, sit on the x-axis a distance $2a$ apart: $+5Q$ at $x=-a$, $-Q$ at $x=+a$, with the midpoint $O$ at the origin. Give answers in terms of $k$, $Q$, $a$.

(a) Force on the $-Q$ charge

Magnitude: $F=k\dfrac{(5Q)(Q)}{(2a)^2}=\dfrac{5kQ^2}{4a^2}$. The charges are opposite, so $-Q$ is attracted toward $+5Q$ (to the left, $-x$).

Answer (a)$\vec F_{-Q}=-\dfrac{5kQ^2}{4a^2}\,\hat i$
(b) Resultant field at midpoint $O$

Each charge is a distance $a$ from $O$. The field of $+5Q$ points away from it → $+x$; the field of $-Q$ points toward it → also $+x$. They add:

$E_O=k\dfrac{5Q}{a^2}+k\dfrac{Q}{a^2}=\dfrac{6kQ}{a^2}$ (in $+x$).

Answer (b)$\vec E_O=\dfrac{6kQ}{a^2}\,\hat i$
(c) Resultant potential at $O$

Potential is a scalar — just add (with signs): $V_O=k\dfrac{5Q}{a}+k\dfrac{-Q}{a}=\dfrac{4kQ}{a}$.

Answer (c)$V_O=\dfrac{4kQ}{a}$
(d) Energy to separate them to infinity

$U=k\dfrac{(5Q)(-Q)}{2a}=-\dfrac{5kQ^2}{2a}$. Work needed $=0-U=\dfrac{5kQ^2}{2a}$.

Answer (d)$W=\dfrac{5kQ^2}{2a}$ (positive — energy must be supplied)

Booklet B (charges $+4Q$ and $-Q$)

(a) $\vec F=-\dfrac{kQ^2}{a^2}\hat i$   (b) $\vec E_O=\dfrac{5kQ}{a^2}\hat i$   (c) $V_O=\dfrac{3kQ}{a}$   (d) $W=\dfrac{2kQ^2}{a}$

Practice

Practice 1

Two point charges $q_1=+3.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ and $q_2=+2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ are $0.20\,\mathrm m$ apart. Find the force between them. (Use $k\approx9.0\times10^9$.)

$F=k\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r^2}=9.0\times10^9\dfrac{(3.0\times10^{-6})(2.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2}$.

$F=\dfrac{5.4\times10^{-2}}{0.04}=1.35\,\mathrm N$ (repulsive).

Answer$F\approx1.35\,\mathrm N$, repulsive.

Why: plug into $F=kq_1q_2/r^2$; like charges repel.

Practice 2

Two equal charges repel each other with $2.5\,\mathrm N$ when $q=5.0\,\mu\mathrm C$. How far apart are they?

$r=\sqrt{\dfrac{kq^2}{F}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(5.0\times10^{-6})^2}{2.5}}$.

$r=\sqrt{0.09}=0.30\,\mathrm m$.

Answer$r=0.30\,\mathrm m$.

Why: solve $F=kq^2/r^2$ for $r$.

Practice 3

Two charges exert a force $F$ on each other. If each charge is tripled and their separation is doubled, by what factor does the force change?

$F\propto\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r^2}$, so the factor is $\dfrac{3\times3}{2^2}=\dfrac{9}{4}$.

AnswerIt increases by a factor of $2.25$.

Why: scale numerator by charges, denominator by distance².

Practice 4

Charges $+2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ (at $x=0$) and $+2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ (at $x=0.10\,\mathrm m$) lie on a line. Find the net force on $+1.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ placed at $x=0.20\,\mathrm m$.

From the far charge ($r=0.20$): $F_1=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(2\times10^{-6})(1\times10^{-6})}{0.04}=0.45\,\mathrm N$.

From the near charge ($r=0.10$): $F_2=\dfrac{0.018}{0.01}=1.8\,\mathrm N$. Both push in $+x$.

Answer$F_\text{net}=2.25\,\mathrm N$ in the $+x$ direction.

Why: superpose collinear forces (add since same direction).

Practice 5

Three $+4.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ charges sit at the corners of an equilateral triangle of side $0.30\,\mathrm m$. Find the magnitude of the net force on one charge.

Each neighbour: $F=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(4\times10^{-6})^2}{(0.30)^2}=1.6\,\mathrm N$.

Two such forces $60^\circ$ apart: $F_\text{net}=2F\cos30^\circ=1.6\sqrt3\approx2.8\,\mathrm N$.

Answer$F_\text{net}\approx2.8\,\mathrm N$, directed away from the triangle's centre.

Why: add the two equal forces as vectors ($60^\circ$ between them).

Practice 6

$+9.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ is at $x=0$ and $+4.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ is at $x=1.0\,\mathrm m$. Where on the line between them is the net force on a test charge zero?

Set fields equal: $\dfrac{9}{x^2}=\dfrac{4}{(1-x)^2}\Rightarrow\dfrac{3}{x}=\dfrac{2}{1-x}$.

$3(1-x)=2x\Rightarrow x=0.60\,\mathrm m$.

Answer$x=0.60\,\mathrm m$ from the $9.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ charge.

Why: balance the two repulsions; the null point sits closer to the smaller charge.

Practice 7

Two identical metal spheres carry $+6.0\,\mu\mathrm C$ and $+2.0\,\mu\mathrm C$. They are touched together and then separated to $0.20\,\mathrm m$. Find the new force.

On contact, charge shares equally: each $=\dfrac{6+2}{2}=4.0\,\mu\mathrm C$.

$F=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(4\times10^{-6})^2}{(0.20)^2}=\dfrac{0.144}{0.04}=3.6\,\mathrm N$.

AnswerEach sphere holds $4.0\,\mu\mathrm C$; $F=3.6\,\mathrm N$.

Why: identical conductors split total charge equally on contact.

Practice 8

Estimate the ratio of the electric to the gravitational force between a proton and an electron (it is independent of separation).

$\dfrac{F_e}{F_g}=\dfrac{kq^2}{Gm_pm_e}=\dfrac{9.0\times10^9(1.6\times10^{-19})^2}{(6.67\times10^{-11})(1.67\times10^{-27})(9.11\times10^{-31})}$.

$\approx\dfrac{2.3\times10^{-28}}{1.0\times10^{-67}}$.

Answer$\dfrac{F_e}{F_g}\approx2\times10^{39}$.

Why: both go as $1/r^2$, so $r$ cancels; electricity is vastly stronger.

Practice 9

Two equal charges $0.50\,\mathrm m$ apart repel with $0.90\,\mathrm N$. Find the charge.

$q=\sqrt{\dfrac{Fr^2}{k}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{0.90(0.50)^2}{9.0\times10^9}}=\sqrt{2.5\times10^{-11}}$.

Answer$q=5.0\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm C=5.0\,\mu\mathrm C$.

Why: invert $F=kq^2/r^2$ for $q$.

Practice 10

$+Q$ is at the origin and $-Q$ is at $x=d$. In which direction is the force on a small positive test charge placed midway between them?

The $+Q$ repels it toward $+x$; the $-Q$ attracts it toward $+x$.

Both contributions point the same way, so they add.

AnswerIn the $+x$ direction (toward the negative charge).

Why: sketch both forces — for a positive test charge they both point toward the $-$ charge.
